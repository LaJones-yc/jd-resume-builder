# Evidence-first JD tailoring

Borrow the useful ideas from general resume-tailoring workflows while adding evidence and bilingual controls.

## Analyze

Group the JD into responsibilities, required capabilities, preferred capabilities, tools/methods, domain vocabulary, and seniority signals. Rank requirements by repetition, placement, and specificity rather than counting keywords alone.

## Match

For each requirement, map one of:

- Direct verified evidence
- Transferable verified evidence
- Weak/indirect evidence
- No evidence

Do not turn weak evidence into direct evidence through wording.

## Select

Choose experiences with the strongest combined relevance, evidence quality, recency, differentiation, and measurable depth. The page is a constrained portfolio, not a chronological dump. Education, internships, research, and projects may be reordered within truthful conventions. Research and projects may merge when the distinction does not help the target role.

Count internship, research, and project entries together. Keep the combined total at 8-9 or fewer on a one-page resume, and usually below that when several entries need detailed bullets. Do not add weak projects merely to reach the cap.

Determine section order from the target role. Rank each section by direct JD relevance, evidence strength, differentiation, and likely recruiter attention. A data-analysis JD may warrant projects before research; a research-heavy JD may warrant research first. Merge research with projects when research has only one entry and a separate heading adds more fragmentation than signal.

Within each project/research section, use reverse chronology by end date and then start date. Change that internal order only at the user's request. Relevance determines inclusion and section placement; it does not override recency inside a section by default.

## Rewrite

- Lead with the aspect most relevant to the JD.
- Use exact JD terminology only when it accurately describes the work.
- Preserve dates, metrics, tools, scope, ownership, and outcome.
- Prefer concrete methods and decisions over generic adjectives.
- Keep enough technical or business detail to make the claim credible.

## ATS and AI-screening coverage

Build a short coverage ledger containing each high-priority JD term, its importance, the supporting experience, evidence level, and the exact resume phrase that covers it. Use four evidence levels: direct, transferable, weak, and none.

- Prefer the employer's exact term when it is a truthful description of verified work.
- Add keywords inside evidence-rich bullets, skills, or role context rather than as detached lists.
- Preserve a user-designated canonical sentence and make the smallest useful edit.
- Use a major term once or twice where it reads naturally; repetition is not evidence.
- Leave unsupported terms uncovered and disclose the gap instead of fabricating familiarity.
- Audit both machine parsing and human readability after drafting: important terms should be extractable as text, but bullets must still explain action, method, and result.

## Handle gaps

Ask for evidence or acknowledge the gap. Never add an unverified skill simply because the JD requests it.

## User checkpoint

Show the proposed experience selection and the highest-impact rewrites before final rendering when user judgment could change the narrative.
